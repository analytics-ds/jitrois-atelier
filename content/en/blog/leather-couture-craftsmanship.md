**Leather couture** describes a way of approaching leather that borrows its codes from haute couture. Far from classic leather goods, it consists of cutting the hide as one cuts a noble fabric, with the same demand for cut, drape and finish. This page details how an exceptional leather garment is born, step by step.

## In brief

1. Leather couture applies the gestures of haute couture to the work of the hide.
2. The making follows four stages: selection, cutting, assembly, finishing.
3. Handwork is essential because leather is a living, irregular material.
4. The quality of the hide determines the entire look of the finished garment.
5. Maison Jitrois places this savoir-faire within French manufacturing.

## Choosing the hide, the first requirement

Everything begins with the **hide**. A leather couture piece tolerates no approximation: the hide must be regular, supple and free of visible flaws. Exceptional houses often work plonge lambskin, prized for its finesse, or hides specially prepared to become [stretch leather]({{< relref lang="en" path="blog/what-is-stretch-leather.md" >}}).

Each hide is unique. The artisan reads its grain, identifies its finest areas and anticipates how it will behave once cut. This reading determines the placement of the pieces and, ultimately, the elegance of the garment.

## Cutting and patterning like in couture

**Cutting** is the most demanding stage. Unlike fabric, leather cannot be unstitched without leaving a mark: every needle hole is permanent. The pattern must therefore be right the first time.

The pieces are placed along the direction of the hide, just as a couturier places pieces along the straight grain of a fabric. This rigour ensures the garment will drape correctly and follow the body, especially on fitted cuts such as [leather trousers]({{< relref lang="en" path="blog/how-to-wear-leather-trousers.md" >}}).

## Assembling and stitching by hand

Then comes **assembly**. The pieces are joined one by one, most often by hand, because leather requires a constant and even tension of the stitch. A thread pulled too tight creases the hide, a thread too loose leaves a slack seam.

This artisanal gesture explains the time spent on each garment. It echoes the story of a house such as the one told in the portrait of [Jean-Claude Jitrois]({{< relref lang="en" path="blog/jean-claude-jitrois-stretch-leather.md" >}}), who made leather a couture material in its own right.

## Finishing, the signature of detail

**Finishing** makes the difference between a good garment and an exceptional piece. Edges, linings, fastenings and topstitching are worked to disappear or, on the contrary, to underline a line. It is in these details that the level of savoir-faire can be read.
